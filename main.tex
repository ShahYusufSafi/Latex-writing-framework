\begin{abstract}
Many sensing devices rely on transduction mechanisms that convert mechanical deformation into measurable electrical signals.
A prominent example is the piezoresistive effect, where the electrical conductivity of a material changes under mechanical strain.

This phenomenon originates from modifications in the underlying electronic transport properties, which can be influenced by lattice deformation, carrier mobility variations, and, in certain regimes, tunneling effects.

This thesis aims to develop a computational framework for modeling strain-dependent conductivity in deformable materials by connecting physical mechanisms at different scales.
At the continuum level, the mechanical behavior is described by the equations of elasticity, while the electrical response is governed by a conductivity equation with strain-dependent coefficients.
This leads to a coupled system of partial differential equations of the form
$$
\nabla \cdot \boldsymbol{\sigma}(u) = 0, \qquad
\nabla \cdot (\sigma(\varepsilon(u)) \nabla V) = 0$$
,where $u$ denotes the displacement field, $V$ the electrical potential, and $\sigma(\varepsilon(u))$ represents an effective conductivity capturing the influence of mechanical deformation.

The conductivity function is treated as an effective quantity, allowing for future incorporation of transport models derived from electronic structure calculations.

Rather than treating this dependence purely phenomenologically, the work discusses how such constitutive relations can be interpreted in terms of underlying electronic transport mechanisms, providing a physically motivated link between microscopic behavior and macroscopic modeling.

From a computational perspective, the study focuses on the formulation and numerical solution of the coupled system, with particular emphasis on flexible representations of the strain–conductivity relationship.
This includes the exploration of hybrid approaches that combine physics-based modeling with data-driven approximations.

The proposed framework aims to provide a consistent and extensible approach for studying electromechanical coupling in materials, with potential applications in sensing technologies, flexible electronics, and structural health monitoring
\end{abstract}
